No. IStrategyState of Fashion 2026

Luxury's recalibration year: trust, not price, is the lever.

McKinsey and BoF's tenth annual State of Fashion finds 80% of 2023–2025 luxury growth came from price hikes rather than volume. The aspirational middle-class consumer has been effectively priced out, and a generation of accessible local challengers in China (Laopu Gold, Songmont) is filling the gap with credible craftsmanship at lower price points.

McKinsey's Gemma D'Auria frames 2026 as "recalibration" driven by what she calls a "breach of trust." Her sharper claim is regional: creativity and innovation matter strongly in China but weakly in Europe — meaning a single global brand-positioning strategy now leaks utility on both ends. Margins, not growth, are the operative variable for executives this year.

A natural setting for a structural demand model with a latent trust state, or a Bayesian-persuasion framing of price-as-quality-signal that backfires when the signal grows too loud for too long. The cross-regional heterogeneity in creativity-as-driver is itself a publishable finding if you can formalize it.
Further Reading Han, Nunes & Drèze (2010), "Signaling Status with Luxury Goods: The Role of Brand Prominence" Journal of Marketing, 74(4), 15–30. The foundational taxonomy of brand-prominence-by-consumer-type — directly relevant to how aspirational consumers being priced out reshapes signaling equilibria. journals.sagepub.com

No. IISignalingQuiet Luxury

The Row, Phoebe Philo, and the logo-free elite.

Andrea Felsted (Bloomberg, via BoF) argues elite spenders are migrating from overexposed mega-brands toward understated, logo-free luxury. The Row and Philo are the canonical exemplars. This is countersignaling in textbook form: those highest in a property invest less in proving it, relying instead on in-group recognition.

The Bellezza & Berger (2020) "Distance" framework is useful here — high-status consumers can safely adopt low-status signals because their position is secure, while middle-status consumers must signal loudly. The Row's appeal is structurally the inverse of Gucci's. For a paper, this is unusually clean: succession events function as natural experiments on logo prominence.

An event study around designer succession or a structural model with three signaling types (low / middle / high), where the high type prefers signal absence. The empirical strategy practically writes itself once you have transactional data from a multi-brand retailer.
Further Reading Feltovich, Harbaugh & To (2002), "Too Cool for School? Signaling and Countersignaling" RAND Journal of Economics, 33(4), 630–649. The foundational countersignaling paper. Read it once and you'll see the structure underneath every "quiet luxury" article you encounter for the next year. wiley.com

No. IIIMarketsQ1 Earnings

Kering Q1, Hermès Q1, and the Tapestry–Kering inversion.

Kering reported Q1 revenue of €3.57B (-6.2% YoY), with Gucci at €1.35B (-14.3%). Hermès posted €4.1B (+5.6%) but missed analyst expectations, citing Middle East disruption. BNP Paribas forecasts ~6% organic growth for the sector in 2026, with the US as the only region carrying both short- and medium-term positive catalysts.

The deeper signal is the Tapestry–Kering inversion: Coach's parent now trades at a market cap nearly matching Kering's. The market is pricing "accessible-premium with strong Gen Z affinity" comparably to traditional ultra-luxury. If that's right, brand-equity-vs.-market-cap divergences become a fertile dependent variable for a paper on what investors actually pay for in fashion conglomerates.

Build a hedonic regression of market cap on brand-attribute vectors (heritage, logo prominence, Gen-Z share, regional exposure). The residual is a measure of "narrative premium" — itself an interesting object to explain.
Further Reading Simon & Sullivan (1993), "The Measurement and Determinants of Brand Equity: A Financial Approach" Marketing Science, 12(1), 28–52. The canonical paper on extracting brand equity from financial market data — methodologically right next to what you'd need for the Kering/Tapestry question. informs.org

No. IVMethodsNBER Working Paper

Ads as cues: a memory-based theory of brand habit.

Bordalo, Burro, and co-authors (NBER WP 34387) develop a model in which advertising operates as a memory cue rather than a marginal-utility shifter. A Pepsi ad reminds Pepsi drinkers of Pepsi but reminds Coke drinkers of Coke — similarity reinforces habit and reduces cross-brand mobility precisely when products are most substitutable.

Two sharp empirical predictions follow: similar ads create negative spillovers via memory interference, and ad effectiveness scales with habit strength. Pairs naturally with Bronnenberg-Dubé's persistent brand-capital framework (NBER WP 22691) — together they form a candidate citation backbone for any branding paper you write in the next two years.

The prediction that ad similarity reinforces habits while reducing cross-brand mobility maps cleanly onto luxury, where category-level visual conventions are exceptionally strong (the campaign aesthetics of Saint Laurent vs. Celine vs. Bottega are convergent). A test using image embeddings to measure ad similarity could be original and tractable.
Further Reading Bronnenberg & Dubé (2017), "The Formation of Consumer Brand Preferences" Annual Review of Economics, vol. 9, 353–382. The companion theoretical apparatus to Bordalo et al. — read together they cover the modern empirical literature on brand capital. nber.org/papers/w22691
Sources
  • Bordalo et al., "Ads as Cues," NBER WP 34387. nber.org
No. VMethodsCausal ML

Causal ML for marketing ROI: two recent frameworks.

Two early-2026 conference papers worth a methodological skim. Marmonier & Aranda (IDEAL 2025, Jan 2026) combine matching, IPW, and causal forests to estimate heterogeneous ATTs on campaign features. Ho & Lam (ICCIES 2026) propose a multi-stage Robust Causal Inference Framework using propensity-score estimation and causal meta-learners — S/T/X-Learner ensembles — to estimate CATE in direct marketing.

What's worth noticing: both are methodologically rigorous on the pre-treatment assumption, which is where most applied marketing work cuts corners. Ho-Lam explicitly excludes "call duration" as a leakage feature. If you want to publish quickly, the framework is a defensible template; if you want to publish well, pair it with a clean economic theory of why heterogeneity exists in your setting.

The literature is hungry for an application that combines meta-learner CATE with a theory of heterogeneity grounded in something other than demographics — e.g., past brand-relationship state, signaling type, or category-level habit strength à la Bordalo.
Further Reading Wager & Athey (2018), "Estimation and Inference of Heterogeneous Treatment Effects Using Random Forests" Journal of the American Statistical Association, 113(523), 1228–1242. The foundational causal forest paper. Every applied CATE paper you'll read cites this; understanding the inference machinery is non-optional. arxiv.org/abs/1510.04342
Sources
  • Marmonier & Aranda (2026), Springer / IDEAL. link.springer.com
  • Ho & Lam (2026), Springer / ICCIES. link.springer.com

No. VIFrontierAI & Discovery

AI shopping agents as a structural disruption.

McKinsey's Anita Balchandani argues consumers are routing discovery through AI assistants, making "generative engine optimization" (GEO) a successor concept to SEO. The question is no longer how Google ranks you but how an LLM represents you. The disruption is more existential for multi-brand aggregators and platforms than for direct-to-consumer brands.

The implications are sharp and underexplored. If discovery shifts from a search-results page to a single LLM-generated recommendation, long-tail brand visibility collapses unless the LLM has been trained on rich, distinctive content about the brand. This is a research frontier with very little canonical theory yet — an opportunity for early entrants.

A first-mover paper could define the field's vocabulary. Audit how a frontier LLM recommends luxury items across structured prompts: are heritage brands over-recommended? Is recency biased? Is there a price-tier ceiling at which LLMs stop differentiating?
Further Reading Brand, Israeli & Ngwe (2023), "Using LLMs for Market Research" Harvard Business School Working Paper 23-062. Establishes how GPT-class models can be used to extract consumer preferences, and explicitly addresses the distributional structure of LLM responses — methodologically essential for any GEO/AI-discovery paper. hbs.edu

No. VIISignalingAesthetic Polarity

Maximalism's commercial resurgence vs. quiet luxury.

QArt Solutions' B2B data shows statement pieces and bold tailoring have moved from niche to core commercial priority in 2026, driven by Gen Z's $400B in identity-signaling spend. Alessandro Michele at Valentino, Jean Paul Gaultier's theatricality, and Comme des Garçons' sculptural excess all confirm the trajectory.

Set this against Item II (logo-free quiet luxury) and you have a textbook Bourdieu-Veblen tension: maximalism for the aspirational, minimalism for the secure elite. Same calendar year, opposite signaling strategies, segmented by consumer type. This is exactly the structure a formal two-type signaling model is built to capture.

A formal signaling model with two consumer types and observable-vs-unobservable luxury cues could publish well in QME or Marketing Science. Bonus: a Distinction-style empirical test using image embeddings of recent runway shows to quantify aesthetic dispersion across price tiers and consumer cohorts.
Further Reading Bellezza, Gino & Keinan (2014), "The Red Sneakers Effect: Inferring Status and Competence from Signals of Nonconformity" Journal of Consumer Research, 41(1), 35–54. A modern empirical anchor for the idea that breaking aesthetic conventions can itself be a status move. Foundational for any quiet-vs-loud paper. academic.oup.com

No. VIIIStructureCircular Economy

Resale, repair, and circularity as a margin lever.

Deloitte's 2026 Global Powers of Luxury reports 68.3% of luxury fashion companies now offer repair/refurbishment, 53.8% run certified pre-owned programs, and 44.5% partner with resale platforms. Euromonitor reframes this as sustainability-as-ROI rather than sustainability-as-compliance — a meaningful shift in how brands rationalize the investment.

For Veblen goods, this is a non-trivial structural change. A controlled resale channel commits a brand to a credible secondary-market price, which feeds back into primary-market willingness-to-pay. The Birkin's price stability across primary and resale is itself a brand asset; what happens when that asset gets actively managed rather than emerging organically?

A two-period model with endogenous resale supply, where the brand chooses primary-market quantity and resale-channel participation jointly. The key tension: more resale availability increases primary-market access (good) but erodes scarcity (bad). The equilibrium balance is publishable.
Further Reading Amaldoss & Jain (2005), "Conspicuous Consumption and Sophisticated Thinking" Management Science, 51(10), 1449–1466. A formal model of Veblen-good pricing under heterogeneous consumer sophistication — the natural baseline before introducing a resale channel. informs.org

No. IXFrontierHBR · Agentic AI

When LLMs miscategorize your brand.

Harvard Business Review (March 2026) reports that two-thirds of Gen Z and over half of Millennials are now using LLMs to research products. Pernod Ricard ran a formal audit of how leading models represented its brands and found systematic mis-categorization — Ballantine's, an affordable mass-market Scotch, was repeatedly classified as a prestige product.

This is the operationalization of Item VI. The empirical fact is that LLMs are now the medium of brand discovery for younger consumers, and they get the brand-tier mapping wrong in non-random ways. For luxury, this matters acutely: prestige is a positional good, and an LLM that flattens the prestige hierarchy reshapes consumer choice sets.

An audit study: for a representative basket of luxury and premium brands, ask a frontier LLM to (a) rank prestige, (b) recommend for an occasion, (c) estimate price. Compare to actual market prices and to expert-rated prestige. The distributional errors are the paper.
Further Reading Li, Zhang, Wang & Gao (2024), "Frontiers: Determining the Validity of Large Language Models for Automated Perceptual Analysis" Marketing Science, 43(4), 665–679. Tests whether LLMs can substitute for survey-based brand perception studies — directly relevant to any audit of LLM-mediated brand discovery. informs.org

No. XMethodsarXiv · LLM Bias

Cultural encoding and the Existence Gap in LLM brand discovery.

A recent arXiv paper introduces the concept of an "Existence Gap" — brands' absence from AI-generated recommendations due to insufficient presence in LLM training data. The empirical claim is striking: Chinese LLMs (Qwen3, DeepSeek, Doubao) recommend brands at 88.9%, vs. 58.3% for international LLMs (GPT, Claude, Gemini) on identical English-language queries. The gap is statistically robust (χ² = 226.60, p < .001).

This is a much harder problem than search engine bias. With search, you can theoretically buy your way in. With LLM training corpora, the brand is either present in the data moat or it isn't — and the asymmetry is locked in by training cycles. For luxury, where Western houses dominate Western corpora, this works in their favor; for emerging-market challenger brands, it's a structural barrier invisible to traditional market-entry analysis.

Two angles: (1) a descriptive paper quantifying the Existence Gap across categories and LLMs, (2) a strategic paper modeling firm investment in "training-data presence" as a new form of marketing capital — distinct from advertising but with similar persistence properties.
Further Reading "Cultural Encoding in Large Language Models: The Existence Gap in AI-Mediated Brand Discovery" (2026) arXiv preprint 2601.00869. The source paper. Worth reading the methodology section closely — the audit-study design is reusable for many adjacent research questions. arxiv.org/pdf/2601.00869
